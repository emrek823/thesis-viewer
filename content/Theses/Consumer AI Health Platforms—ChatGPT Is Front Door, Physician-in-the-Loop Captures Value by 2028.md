## The Counter Thesis

**The strongest counter-argument is that AI will increasingly prescribe without physicians, and OpenAI will go direct.** Utah's Doctronic pilot proves autonomous prescribing is legally and technically possible. If autonomous prescribing expands beyond refills to new prescriptions and controlled substances, the physician-in-the-loop monetization layer collapses. Doctronic expects "a dozen states" to approve similar programs in 2026, and the federal Healthy Technology Act (H.R.238) would enable AI prescribing if FDA-approved.

**But this requires:**
1. Regulatory approval for new prescriptions (not just refills—currently excluded everywhere)
2. Controlled substance authorization (explicitly excluded from Utah pilot and all proposed legislation)
3. Liability framework for AI-only decisions (Florida court ruling makes AI chatbots "products"—the opposite of liability-free)
4. Consumer trust in AI without physician backup (currently at 39%; women at 31%)

The second counter is that the lawsuit wave could trigger regulatory crackdown. Eight wrongful death and product liability cases against OpenAI demonstrate real harm. New York passed AI companion regulation effective November 2025. If ChatGPT Health causes a high-profile death from health-specific advice, the "information only, not clinical" positioning collapses.

The third counter is that OpenAI for Healthcare competes with K Health for health system contracts. OpenAI launched HIPAA-compliant enterprise tools with Cedars-Sinai, HCA, UCSF partnerships. But this makes OpenAI an infrastructure provider (like Epic)—K Health's AI Physician Mode is the application layer on top. K Health's Mayo Clinic partnership gives access to 2,500+ PCPs; OpenAI competes on infrastructure, not care delivery.

**For this thesis to be wrong:** (1) Utah-style autonomous prescribing expands to new prescriptions AND controlled substances, OR (2) ChatGPT Health wrongful death lawsuit creates precedent that collapses consumer AI entirely, OR (3) OpenAI acquires a physician-in-the-loop company and goes direct (they bought Torch for $100M—patient records, not care delivery).
