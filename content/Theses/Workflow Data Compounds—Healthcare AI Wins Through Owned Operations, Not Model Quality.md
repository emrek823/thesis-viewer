**Consensus believes:** AI model quality determines winners. Better algorithms, larger training sets, more parameters. The best model wins the market.

**Actually:** AI model quality is converging. 85% of healthcare AI spend flows to startups because they ship faster—but ambient documentation is already commoditizing (67% of outpatient providers would switch vendors). The actual commercial moat is **workflow depth**: companies that start with a wedge (documentation, referrals, voice) and expand across the value chain (coding, prior auth, revenue cycle) create switching costs that persist even as the underlying AI becomes commodity.

**In 5 years:** Market bifurcates. Vertical Operating System Players (Abridge, Tennr, Assort) own clinical workflows and survive Epic bundling through platform expansion. M&A-Built Platforms (PE roll-ups like New Mountain's "Smarter Technologies") acquire at services multiples and transform to software economics. Point solutions that never expanded beyond their initial wedge before Epic/horizontal platforms bundle the feature—they die or get acquired for parts.

## Investment Take

**Today:** The question for healthcare AI in 3-5 years isn't "who has the best model?" or "who has the best channel partners?" It's **who owns the workflow data that compounds?**

Three mechanisms explain why workflow ownership beats model quality:

**Mechanism 1: Compounding Data Loops.** Every patient interaction, every referral processed, every claim coded becomes training data. Tennr achieved "300-400% efficiency improvement" not because their AI is fundamentally better, but because they've processed millions of faxes across hundreds of payer-provider combinations. SimonMed's CIO notes: "no player outside the radiology space has taken the time to understand the radiology landscape and ecosystem." That understanding IS the data.

**Mechanism 2: Integration Depth Creates Switching Costs.** SleepQuest's Tennr integration took 7 months vs. 3-month expectation because "they had to play with ECW and get them to agree to certain things." Pinnacle Group chose Tennr specifically because "they had a lot of experience API into Brightree... other guys said 'Yes, we can learn that'... It's really not something you learned." This integration expertise is earned through painful deployments.

**Mechanism 3: Platform Expansion Before Commoditization.** Abridge expanded from documentation → revenue cycle intelligence → prior authorization → coding in 2025. Their $5.3B valuation reflects this expansion, not documentation quality. The platform expansion IS the survival strategy.

**MAJOR THREAT (2026 REBUILD):** Epic's native AI scribe "Art" launches early 2026 at rumored $80/provider/month vs. startup ~$600/month. Combined with 67% willingness to switch among outpatient providers, this is existential for documentation point solutions. 2026 is the year "good enough" incumbent AI wipes out "best of breed" point solutions.

**The counter is platform expansion.** Abridge raised $550M in 2025 specifically to escape the documentation kill zone. Zachary Lipton (Abridge CTO): "Healthcare has long been the land of a thousand point solutions, and I think that structure is going to begin to collapse in 2026."

**In 3-5 years:** The healthcare AI market bifurcates.

**Winners:**

## The Counter Thesis

**The counter-thesis:** Workflow ownership is a temporary advantage in a market where AI performance converges and incumbents can acquire workflow data through their distribution.

Epic has 30 years of healthcare experience, 42% hospital market share, and sees every Abridge deployment through their Workshop program. When Epic's native AI scribe works "good enough" AND they observe workflow patterns across their install base AND they use that data to improve bundled offerings—startup workflow data moats erode.

**What would need to be true for this counter-thesis to win:**
1. Epic's native AI achieves "good enough" quality AND adoption exceeds 50% within 18 months
2. Horizontal players (Sierra, Reducto) successfully move into clinical workflows
3. Workflow data proves less defensible than expected—new entrants match incumbent data within 12-18 months

**Why I still believe workflow wins:**
1. Epic's Art is documentation-focused. Revenue cycle, prior auth, coding expansion requires different data and workflows.
2. "Good enough" doesn't win specialty depth—28-day retina injection rules, DME fax processing, radiology referral complexity
3. Integration expertise takes years to accumulate. "It's not something you learned."
4. Platform expansion is already underway—by the time Epic bundles docs, Abridge owns too much to rip out

**The bull thesis bet:** Workflow data compounds faster than incumbents can catch up—by the time Epic bundles documentation, Abridge owns prior auth AND coding AND revenue cycle, making the documentation feature a trojan horse for platform stickiness.
