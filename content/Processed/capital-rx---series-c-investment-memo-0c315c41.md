## Problem
Pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) are organizations that manage pharmacy benefit claims for employers and health plans. Their responsibilities include developing formularies (approved medication lists), negotiating drug prices (including rebates and discounts) with manufacturers, contracting directly with individual pharmacies, and managing utilization through tools like prior authorization. As a result, PBMs have substantial influence over the drugs that its members use and the price at which those members pay. In theory, the group purchasing dynamics of PBMs should mean they are able to negotiate larger discounts for their member populations, driving down drug costs for its patients.

However, like other areas in healthcare, the pharmaceutical value chain is one of misaligned incentives. This is because PBMs can generate substantial profit off the following:
1) **Spread pricing**: The difference between how much PBMs charge employers and health plans and how much they pay manufacturers / pharmacies. Historically, drug pricing has been a black box to everyone but the PBMs. Employers are in the dark about how much PBMs reimburse pharmacies, and pharmacies are similarly unaware of what PBMs charge employers and health plans. As a result of this information asymmetry, PBMs are able to pocket the difference.
2) **Rebates**: PBM-negotiated discounts offered by drug manufacturers for branded prescriptions. Traditionally, PBMs have retained a portion of these rebates for themselves - meaning the savings do not accrue to its fullest extent to the end payers (employers, health plans, and ultimately the patient). Furthermore, there is little transparency on exactly how much these rebates are, and employers and health plans typically receive rebates in an aggregated check, with few details on associated patients, prescriptions, and amounts. Some also argue that rebates incentivize PBMs to prioritize higher priced drugs over more cost-effective ones or generics, even if demonstrated clinical outcomes are equal.

On top of the above, PBMs typically also charge an administrative fee to employers and health plans for overall management, but this is a small portion of their revenue model.

Due to these practices, drug pricing in the U.S. has become highly variable and opaque. Furthermore, the PBM landscape is currently an oligopoly, with the 'Big 3' (UnitedHealthcare's Optum Rx, CVS Caremark, and Cigna's Express Scripts) controlling ~80% of the claims market. Due to their substantial negotiating and pricing leverage, the Big 3 are not incentivized to lift the veil on their practices any time soon, as their PBM segments have become a significant source of value creation for the parent companies. PBM segments made up ~40% and ~20% of operating income for CVS and UnitedHealthcare, respectively, in 2021.

Lastly, despite increased public scrutiny around PBMs and their practices, policy reforms in this space will likely be limited to government programs like Medicare Part D and Medicaid and general transparency initiatives, with Commercial markets largely left alone. The rebate rule" proposed by the Trump administration (which would have required all rebates in Medicare Part D to be passed through to the patient at the point of sale) has been delayed until at least 2026 because it would cause federal costs to increase and premiums to rise. ## Capital Rx Solution / Thesis

### PBM
Capital Rx is a 'transparent' PBM, which means the company passes through all rebates and discounts to its employer customers and only captures an administrative fee. As part of their 'Clearinghouse' model, the company leverages publicly available National Average Drug Acquisition Cost (NADAC) data from the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) to ensure their customers secure the lowest cost. ## Market & Commercial
There are an estimated ~6.5B pharmacy claims processed each year in the U.S., and secondary sources size the PBM market at ~$450-500B (Capital Rx considers their PBM and PBA market size to be ~$582B). This is also a space that is expected to grow yearly in the high single digits moving forward, as drug procurement and management remains a complex space to navigate. Any organization that manages pharmacy benefits for covered individuals - whether as a fully insured employer, a health plan servicing Government populations, etc. - needs to have either external services to support this type of work (Capital Rx's PBM customers) or the in-house solutions, teams, and capabilities to do so (Capital Rx's PBA customers).

### PBM
Employers have two options with their pharmacy benefits: a carve-in vs. carve-out plan. In a carve-in plan, pharmacy benefits are bundled into a medical plan so that the medical health plan vendor has the PBM relationship (e.g., Aetna would use in-house CVS Caremark; a few Blues plans are contracted with Prime Therapeutics). Alternatively, in a carve-out plan, employers are able to contract directly with PBMs, enabling greater freedom of choice and control. Contracts in the space typically span three years, and most employers will decide to run a RFP at the end for medical and pharmacy benefits; however, these relationships are highly sticky with industry renewal rates at 3-4%.

Historically, Capital Rx has found success with mid-sized employers that haven't gotten the same attention from the Big 3. But they've started to compete (and win) against the Big 3 in multiple RFPs. In the employer space, current clients include Altec, Subaru, CubeSmart, and Sutter Home. Union/public customers include Communication Workers of America, Philadelphia Federation of Teachers, Bucks County, and City of Hoboken. Lastly, Capital Rx has also sold into health systems, including Baptist Health, St. Luke's University Health Network, Capital Region Medical Center, and Griffin Health.

Apart from direct to employer sales efforts, another channel in this space is selling through benefits brokers and consultants. These entities will negotiate contracts on behalf of employers and advise them on plan selection. Willis Tower Watson, one of the largest benefits consultants in the U.S., recently added Capital Rx to its list of preferred PBM partners, which up until now had only included the Big 3. This represents a significant milestone for the Capital Rx team from a recognition standpoint and is also a more efficient channel for their enterprise sales team.

### PBA
Capital Rx directly sells JUDI to health plans. Though switching back-end architecture is a significant lift, Capital Rx has found incredible traction with newer plans such as Friday Health Plan and Firefly, and have won over select Blues Plans via contracts with Evio and Prime Therapeutics, which is the next largest PBM after the Big 3. The recently signed Prime LOI includes Prime's Commercial and Government lines of business - Commercial (~400K lives) would be worth ~$10M ARR and phased from 1/1/23  12/31/23; Government (~3.5M lives) would be worth $50M+ ARR and would start 1/1/24.

Another recent win is CDPHP, which represents ~340K new lives (170K Commercial, 120K Medicaid, 50K Medicare) that will go live at the beginning of next year. This is a significant win, as Capital Rx displaced their 16-year incumbent CVS Caremark.

That said, GC Base case takes a more conservative view on operating leverage and reduces YoY efficiency gains, especially in Payroll and Benefits.

Assuming Capital Rx can continue its impressive growth with wins against the Big 3, onboard major customers such as Evio and Prime Therapeutics, and continue to grow their book of business from commercial to take on more government plans as well, we believe the Company can comfortably achieve the GC Base case scenario with upside in the form of new products to support their PBM / PBA customers as well as a shift to support medical claims as a TPA.

## Market Landscape / Competitive Analysis
### PBM
The PBM space today is highly consolidated, with the Big 3 (UNH's OptumRx, CVS Caremark, and Cigna's Express Scripts) processing ~80% of total scripts in the U.S. These three are also vertically integrated with other parts of their businesses, creating high value around keeping members within their ecosystems.

The remainder of the market is comprised of legacy mid-mid-majors, like Prime Therapeutics, Humana, MedImpact, etc. (~17% of market), followed by newcomers like Capital Rx. As public scrutiny around the Big 3 has increased due to conversations around price transparency, as well as the increased popularity of self-funded employers, PBM players that rely on a pass-through model have gained traction.

In conversations with two of Capital Rx's PBM customers (Teamsters of Philadelphia and St. Luke's), a few recurring themes stood out:
*   **True, Transparent Cost Savings**: Both customers cited measurable savings from their switch to Capital Rx (for Teamsters, estimated ~$3.5-5M savings and a prescription drug trendline that dropped from predicted 6% to actual 1.6%; for St. Luke's, reduced trendline from double digits to single digits). However, it should also be noted that the magnitude of cost savings is not an easy metric to assess given how opaque their prior contracts were.

*   **Differentiated Customer Service Levels**: Especially compared to their larger PBM competitors, Capital Rx stands out for its top-tier customer service. For Teamsters in particular, providing strong benefits to union members is critical due to union political dynamics, and the team was not satisfied with the level of customer service received from their previous PBM, CVS Caremark. Teamsters has not received many complaints about Capital Rx (a metric of success for them), and when complaints or issues do arise, the Capital Rx team (even AJ) is especially responsive and timely.
*   **Strength of Platform**: Although PBM customers do not directly interact with JUDI, the strength of JUDI's capabilities translates materially to PBM customer satisfaction. For St. Luke's, the fact that Capital Rx has JUDI means that they are able to implement exceptions and custom configurations in a nimble and efficient way - in their words, the execution time frame is unparalleled." Furthermore, though St. Luke's was not actively looking for a new PBM (were satisfied with their existing one, Geisinger), Capital Rx stood out in its ability to provide data in a transparent and granular way off the JUDI platform - something St. Luke's had not been able to obtain in prior relationships.
*   **Level of Knowledge**: In an industry as complex as this one, PBM customers would like their PBMs to be trusted, smart partners. Both Teamsters and St. Luke's cited Capital Rx as highly knowledgeable about the space; in fact, St. Luke's said that Capital Rx's level of expertise was unmatched and that they asked questions no other PBM partners had asked. This is especially high praise given the level of knowledge that St. Luke's (who has some in-house pharmacy assets) itself has.

Furthermore, St. Luke's noted that if the Capital Rx team ventured into the TPA space, they would certainly take a look at the offering.

### PBA
JUDI competes against legacy software players like Laker (now owned by Rite Aid) and RxClaim (owned by Optum). Both systems are decades old (RxClaim was built in 1993, Laker was built in 2004), coded using older languages, and take longer to deploy and push out changes. By contrast, JUDI is a cloud-based platform with a natural language interface that can set up or change plans in a matter of hours.

## Key Risks
*   **Government Adoption of PBA Solution**: Management projects significant growth in the PBA Government segment (Medicaid and Medicare) going forward. This segment is more heavily regulated and complex for administrators than Commercial, so Capital Rx will have to demonstrate longer term success in execution for other Government buyers to gain confidence in their product. Though the company has some proof points for Government success (e.g., launching in Medicaid this year with ~2K lives, recent CDPHP win of ~120K Medicaid and ~50K Medicare lives), this line of business is still in its early days. However, if Capital Rx succeeds, the company will be able to serve all lines of business, increasing its value prop for plan customers.
*   **Scalability of Operations**: Capital Rx's current customers cite the first-class customer service and hands-on support that they receive from the team. While this is a highly differentiated and valuable experience compared to competitors, there are open questions around how scalable this high-touch, pharmacist-driven model is as the company grows and whether Capital Rx can continue to maintain the high level of service that sets it apart.
*   **Response from Big 3**: If the Big 3 feel sufficiently threatened, they may begin to use their volume leverage to compete with Capital Rx on price, while continuing to market the value prop of other in-house capabilities. Additionally, given their resources at hand, it's not out of the question that the Big 3 may build their own modernized adjudication platform in-house. However, other players have tried to build this technology in-house, with limited success; after trying to bring drug purchasing functions in-house in 2018 (including an investment in PBM RxAdvance), Centene has decided to seek an external PBM by 2024. Additionally, despite their sizes, the Big 3's tech infrastructure is still lacking in sophistication - Optum is said to have at least three separate systems from its various acquisitions and is still working through consolidation and migration, with limited headspace dedicated to thinking about standing up an entirely new system in-house.
*   **Legislation that Restricts PBMs**: Increased public scrutiny around PBMs and their practices have triggered conversations around policy reforms in this space. There are a number of actions that the federal and state governments could take, from limiting the ability of PBMs to retain rebates (which would make them more closely resemble Capital Rx's transparent, pass-through model) to

completely getting rid of them. However, our market research and conversations with policy experts indicate that substantial reform in this area will be limited; any legislation that does pass will also likely apply to government programs only. Historical precedent on this issue supports this as well (e.g., delay of Trump administration's proposed 'rebate rule' until at least 2026), especially as lawmakers continue to debate the federal economic consequences of such actions. In short, PBMs are here to stay and any shifts in policy may prove favorable to Capital Rx given they already align with NADAC pricing.
