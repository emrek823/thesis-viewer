# CEO Summary - Nov ‚Äò25 and Full-Year Performance

## Introduction
Our goal entering the final months of 2025 is to reach positive unit economics by closing case-rate contracts, scaling EMS call volume, and continuing to reduce Care Team cost per episode. Based on our current forecast, we expect to turn gross profit positive once BCBS Texas, BCBS Georgia, and United contracts in Texas and Georgia are executed, and we sustain an average of about 100 activations per day. We are not there yet, but we are close on both fronts. BCBS Texas has been approved, and BCBS Georgia and United Texas are in late-stage approval, with executive sponsors pushing toward a January 1 effective date. EMS call volume continues to grow across our core markets as we strengthen EMS account management execution. What follows summarizes what worked in 2025, what slowed us down, and why 2026 will be defined more by execution than by strategy.

## November Results
In November, RightSite delivered 1,246 total activations and 624 diversions. Payor diversion rates held at 82%, and EMS redirect rates were 81%, showing clinical performance remained steady as volume increased. Patient acceptance rates remained above 90% across dispatch and field encounters, and patient satisfaction scores stayed high. Patient care costs declined as Care Team operations improved. Navigator overtime fell 41% with better scheduling, while navigator efficiency increased from 0.9 to 2.3 episodes/hr, a 155% gain, and physician efficiency increased 25%, from 4 to 5 episodes/hr, driven by stronger physician‚Äìnavigator alignment. These operating improvements translated into material Q3‚ÄìQ4 episode cost reductions, including a 66% drop in urgent care cost per episode, a 40% reduction in transportation spend, and a 45% reduction in prescription spend.

## 2025 Year-in-Review Performance
From January through November, RightSite delivered nearly 12,000 activations and more than 6,300 diversions. About 9,000 episodes avoided an ambulance transport, generating more than $21M in EMS savings and over $23M in combined taxpayer and health plan savings.

Performance remained consistent across markets. EMS redirect rates averaged above 80%, and total diversion rates held near 60%.

Care Team operations remained within target ranges. Time-to-answer, time-to-physician, and EMS release metrics stayed within target ranges, while follow-up reach rates averaged around 70%. Time-and-motion work, staffing refinements, and more selective use of SDOH resources reduced cost per activation. We still have work to do, but cost per episode is clearly manageable with discipline.

## What Worked and What Didn‚Äôt
What worked in 2025 was the operating model. EMS interest accelerated across Texas, Georgia, and Florida, and our reputation as a dependable partner drove inbound demand. We also began taking share from Tele911, confirming our differentiation is showing up in the market.

Our payer value proposition sharpened. We shifted to unit-based analysis, leaned into EMS density, moved contracting conversations to state-level teams, and engaged third-party actuarial validation. Together, these changes reduced friction for payer teams and improved late-stage momentum.

# CEO Summary - Nov ‚Äò25 and Full-Year Performance

## What Worked and What Didn‚Äôt (con‚Äôt)

What did not work as well was we missed the gross profit profitability target. Payer contracting cycles ran longer than planned, delaying case-rate execution. EMS sales and go-lives outpaced account management capacity, stretching a lean account management team. Physician licensing and credentialing difficulties delayed collections and complicated scheduling. Limited engineering capacity slowed product work that would otherwise help reduce costs faster. None of these issues change the model, but all affect timing and achieving our gross profit profitability target. The core risks for 2026 remain payer contract timing and the pace at which EMS agencies can be implemented to increase call volume. As scale increases, execution risk rises, particularly given the real-time, service-based nature of what we deliver.

At the same time, external tailwinds are increasing. EMS and ER systems face growing financial pressure as ambulance and ER economics worsen, and the number of uninsured and underinsured patients grows. Congress‚Äô failure to extend ACA subsidies is likely to intensify that pressure, accelerating demand for alternatives to the automatic 911-to-ER pathway for non-emergent care.

## Looking Ahead to 2026

The competitive landscape is also shifting. Tele911 and MedAlly appear less active, and we are already taking share from Tele911 in Texas. EMS agencies consistently describe clear operational and clinical differences in working with RightSite. GMR‚Äôs nurse line remains a potential competitor, but EMS agencies report low re-direction rates, creating an opening for deeper engagement with GMR in 2026.

We‚Äôve built a 2026 forecast model designed to support disciplined scaling while protecting cash. It assumes daily activations rising from roughly 65 in December 2025 to 157 by December 2026, with diversion rates holding near 58%, consistent with 2025 performance. The model focuses on Texas and Georgia, where EMS density and payer engagement are strongest. Florida, the walk-in service line, and expanded Foundation activity for uninsured costs are excluded from the forecast to avoid overpromising on revenue. Each represents potential upside, but none is required to achieve positive gross profit.

Taken together, 2026 looks less like a year of proving the model and more like a year where execution determines outcomes. If we execute well, RightSite has the opportunity to become the category leader in redefining non-emergent 911 and ER care.

Execution in 2026 comes down to three priorities: closing and activating case-rate contracts, scaling EMS go-lives in large MSAs, and continuing to reduce Care Team cost per episode through staffing mix with RNs and APPs to improve physician efficiency, improved workflow, and leveraging RightSite Connect, AI and other technology platforms. At this stage, success depends more on execution than on a new strategy.

# Notes on 2026 Budget

For 2026, our central challenge is balancing accelerating EMS volume with the unpredictability of the payor sales cycles. Our accelerating traction with certain key payors in the 4Q:25 should set us up for some key contract additions in 1H:26. The EMS sales team is in advanced contracting discussions with Austin, Dallas, El Paso agencies in TX and Gwinnett in GA. We have intentionally paced the rollout of these four large agencies across Q2‚ÄìQ4 2026 to manage operational risk and preserve financial discipline.

We have used conservative implementation dates to allow time for United GA, Cigna, Superior, Oscar, and Aetna to come online, protecting the GP profitability target. We are currently targeting a soft date of August 1 for weekends, but this will be dependent on the key metrics that get us to GP profitability.

All of this needs to be balanced with our number one priority: cash. Hiring will be thoughtful with a focus on revenue-generating spend, combined with some needed G&#x26;A to support the activities.
