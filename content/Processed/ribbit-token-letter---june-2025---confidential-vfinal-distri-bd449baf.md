## The Token Revolution, In Finance and Beyond

Over the past 15 years, we have witnessed enormous strides in global financial innovation. Thanks to the growth of the mobile internet ‚Äì and an amazing cohort of entrepreneurs ‚Äì most people around the world now have digital money in their pockets.

But there is so much more to do! We started Ribbit to back rebels fighting a status quo that is still alive and well. While financial access is broader than ever, true personalization is still a luxury afforded to a privileged few. This often means the difference between prosperity and poverty; it is one thing to get a loan, an entirely different thing to get help figuring out if you should take that loan. For the past ten years, we have been asking questions like "what share of the world can access dollars, gold, bitcoin, treasuries, or a global equity index?‚Äù; in the next ten, we need to be asking "what share of the world has access to personal agents working 24x7 to manage their financial risks, cash flows, and returns?"

Fortunately, we are starting to see cracks form in the barriers that the status quo uses to impede progress ‚Äì barriers like information asymmetry, standards incompatibility, regulatory capture, and FUD. And the Token Revolution is poised to break the dam. In LLMs, we now have computers that can capture context, comprehend the world, and write software. In blockchains and modern payment schemes, we have networks that allow machines to issue and move assets around the world instantly. At their core, these systems all run on tokens. If mobile was the answer to what was needed most for the last decade of innovation, tokens are what is needed most for the next decade.

These tokens ‚Äì and the digital agents they will power ‚Äì will fuel an explosion of intelligence that will transform our relationship with technology. We will go from figuring out how technology can help us, to having technology figure it out for us (and then do it). The shape of this future will, without a doubt, astound us, but we feel confident about a few things:

1.  Every person will have tens or hundreds of agents working for them. Anyone with an internet connection will have access to services only available today to the rich and powerful. These agents will help make sense of a noisy world and turn our data into an asset that works for us.
2.  Every business will have tens or hundreds of agents ‚Äì per human employee. The capacity of motivated teams to impact the world will shock us. With falling transaction and information costs, the nature of the firm will change. More than we expect, we will work with and for agents.
3.  Every industry will be radically reshaped by tokens and agents. How and where value is created and preserved within industries will change. Data will progress from being one asset to the asset that matters the most. Brands will still be vital, but the character of brand will evolve.

We always lead with finance because we love it ‚Äì we believe that making money better makes society better. And financial services is the perfect target in an age of intelligent machines. ## Understanding Tokenization (a.k.a. What Are We Talking About Here?)

Tokens are not new. The first ones we know about ‚Äì small tokens made of clay in Mesopotamia during the Neolithic era ‚Äì showed up a bit shy of 10,000 years ago. These tokens were about a third of an inch, worked into one of a dozen different shapes, embellished with lines or pellets, baked in a hearth, and used to express quantities of good. As far as we gather, they were used as money and represented the earliest portable forms of accounting. Especially important tokens could be locked by fire inside envelopes called ‚Äúbullae,‚Äù an ancient kind of encryption. Even then, tradeoffs needed to be managed between privacy and efficiency, and data and money were intricately linked.

These days, tokens are showing up all over the place, and we can describe just about anything in terms of tokens. The Declaration of Independence is 1,627 tokens. The U.S. constitution is about 5,500 tokens. The Bitcoin whitepaper is around 4,330 tokens, describing a system that will only ever produce 21M Bitcoin tokens. Gemini 2.5 Pro has a context window capable of holding 1M tokens, and ChatGPT was trained on something like 300B tokens. Over 40K tokens were launched on Solana every day in May. Over 1.3B Aadhaar identity tokens have been issued in India, and over 817M Pix payment key tokens have been created in Brazil. Mastercard processes about 4B monthly tokens. OpenRouter ‚Äì an interface for accessing multiple LLMs ‚Äì now processes over 5T tokens a month.

Of course, not all these tokens are comparable. Tokens and tokenization have varying meanings based on the context. The first use in computer science shows up in 1950s NLP research, but since then "token" has become a popular digital moniker. If you're talking to an AI researcher, tokenization is the process of breaking text into smaller units that can be words, parts of words, or individual characters. A crypto or capital markets pro would describe tokenization as creating a digital asset on a blockchain, like an NFT (or a digital entitlement to another asset originally created off-chain, like a stablecoin). A payments or data security geek would view a token as an identifier substituted for sensitive data (like a card PAN) to prevent the underlying information from being exposed during transactions.

So how do we make sense of these different definitions? To us, the unifying thread is machine legibility; broadly construed, tokenization is the process of rendering the world for computers. When you hear us talk about tokenizing data or identity or money or assets, we mean making what people know and do across human operating systems (finance, commerce, healthcare, and more) safely accessible to software ‚Äì including to increasingly autonomous software. Since AI systems are trained on and fueled by data about the world, tokens are effectively both the DNA of and feedstock for AI.

Making sense of tokens ‚Äì rehashing taxonomies and relative weights or relationships ‚Äì will be constant work in the years ahead. But the root question will always be the same: what is valuable? Every token type will have their own supply and demand curves, influenced by a variety of factors; for example, how personal (spending history vs. generic preferences), rich (‚Äúchecked-in at 16th St. Equinox‚Äù vs. "flew to NY‚Äù), fresh (real-time vs. last week), verifiable (sensor data vs. self-reported), or reusable (financial account token vs. bank screenshot) are the tokens? And none of this will be static. Raw tokens (medical images) will be continuously refined into value-added tokens (readings) and combined with others (family or genetic data) to create yet more valuable tokens (accurate diagnoses).

All this token creation, refining, and combining will happen in **token factories**, the technologies and organizations that transform tokens of all types into more valuable tokens ‚Äì or, said more conventionally, into higher levels of intelligence. Every software business ‚Äì and, increasingly, other businesses too ‚Äì can be modeled as supplying, building or managing token factories. Some factory complexes are generic but extremely powerful, like the large foundation models; these handle a wide range of inputs and produce an endless range of valuable outputs. Other factories might be deeply specialized, built using unique training data ‚Äì say, for example, a model trained on proprietary material science data by an industrial company to do a highly specific job.

This vast universe of token factories will be utilized by (and often built by) **agents** and **agentic systems**. While there are hundreds of competing definitions of AI agents right now, we believe the term is best used liberally: agents are software systems that can perceive their environment, make

## Why Now?

We could have written this letter over the past couple of years or in a couple of years from now. So why is it burning in our minds now?

### The cost of running token factories is plummeting

Until recently, operating cutting-edge token factories required expensive, specialized infrastructure. Today, a quick glance at Twitter or OpenRouter gives you a sense for the growing abundance of models and compute resources. With intense competition between the world's best capitalized companies, the rise of open-source models like Llama and DeepSeek, and advances in hardware and optimization techniques, the cost of inference has dropped by a factor of 1,000 over three years. As a result, founders of all stripes can now use LLMs to do more complex tasks without breaking the bank.

### The cost of building token factories is plummeting, too

The infrastructure for building token factories is getting radically easier to use. Open-source frameworks like LangChain, AutoGen, and CrewAI can compress months of engineering into a few lines of Python. Vector databases, orchestration tools, and memory modules are increasingly plug-and-play. AI coding agents like Cursor and Devin are spreading like wildfire ‚Äì at leading AI companies, most new code is now LLM-written. And vibe coding is in vogue; platforms like Replit and Genspark are making agents accessible to non-technical users. More founders are experimenting faster than ever before.

### The availability of raw inputs for token factories is exploding

There has never been more unstructured data available, or more ways to capture it. Enterprise systems, public APIs, and real-world signals are increasingly accessible, indexable, and ingestible. AI-powered transcription services like Sonix sell at <$0.20 per minute (5-25x cheaper than human transcription). Synthetic datasets are starting to supplement human-labeled ones, including in areas like coding and customer support. Companies like Scale and Gretel keep improving quality and efficiency. As the costs of storage, bandwidth, and data fall, the range of token inputs keeps expanding.

### Access to critical infrastructure for token factories is growing

Blockchains and modern payment networks are becoming viable tools for a wider range of users and uses. The cost of issuing a global, programmable asset is approaching zero; launching a token on Solana or Base now costs less than $10 in gas, and transaction fees have collapsed to under $0.02. Other modern payment networks and providers ‚Äì from Visa to NPCI (who operates India's UPI) to Stripe in the US ‚Äì are iterating quickly on infrastructure for agents and agentic systems.

### The factory builders are emerging in full force

Entrepreneurial spirit is out in full force. Just as past entrepreneurs built early desktop/web/mobile apps on top of PCs/browsers/smartphones, a new generation of founders are building token factories on top of LLMs. Over 200,000 developers have experimented with agent frameworks in the past year, and the fastest-growing open-source repos on GitHub are related to LLM orchestration. Over 2/3 of YC companies in the last couple of batches are AI-focused. Every week we see more evidence of startups capturing user tokens and attention faster than incumbents can figure out how to tokenize their data.

### The race is on at every part of the stack

The hyperscalers are racing to outspend each other in compute (e.g. Google and Microsoft together are processing over 500T tokens a month). AI data center specialists like Crusoe and CoreWeave are racing to lock up as much power as possible. Leading model companies are racing to win consumer loyalty and usage. Agentic applications of all kinds are racing to collect queries, context, and memory. Irrespective of how much capital ends up incinerated in the frenzy, remarkable new brands and franchises will find their footing and trillions of dollars of equity value will shift in the years ahead.

## Unassuming tokenizers will turn out to be some of AI's first killer apps

Many of history's most ambitious builders have been obsessed with mapping the world: Napoleon refused to travel without his own topographical engineers; Jay Gould developed an early passion for surveying that helped him assemble the most valuable rail network of his era; Sam Walton flew his own plane over small towns to better understand retail patterns and plot Walmart's expansion. By zealously collecting and organizing data, they were able to build systems others could not see. The same instinct is alive and well in many of the best founders we're meeting today. But instead of mapping the world for humans, they are busy mapping human systems for machines.

With all the talk about a world awash in data, it is easy to forget how much still goes uncaptured. One of our favorite areas, wealth management, offers an intuitive example. On the one hand, modern brands like Robinhood or Groww know a lot about you from your ever-growing stream of transactions and clicks. On the other, in twenty minutes of conversation a human advisor can develop a much richer picture of you ‚Äì your family story, goals, personality, fears, and preferences. And this pattern shows up in nearly every personal and professional use case. We have mapped a speck of what will be tokenized.

Many of AI's early killer apps will start this modestly, just capturing latent data; like maps, they will appear unassuming but will deliver immediate value. We have been calling these apps "tokenizers,‚Äù since their initial hero feature is simply grabbing tokens and turning them into relevant context. Note-taking agents like Granola, Otter, or Fireflies are a straightforward example. The first experience can be magical; by converting audio recordings of meetings into structured notes, these apps generate 10x-100x more shareable context. This simple thing creates the foundation a wide range of organizational improvements ‚Äì management teams coordinating better, sales leaders developing teams more effectively, product learning from customers faster, and a lot more. We are seeing a lot of vertical apps starting with the same approach, too. In wealth, for instance, tools like Jump and Zocks are gaining rapid adoption with advisors by turning client conversations into detailed CRM entries and follow ups.

We also need tokenizers for all the data that exists, just not in forms machines can use effectively. When we talk to large enterprises, it often comes back to this; they tout petabytes of data and big AI visions on analyst calls, but in practice that data is buried in poorly structured databases and unstructured documents. Meanwhile, the average knowledge worker still does over 1,000 copy-paste actions a week. While helping companies digitize documents has been a hard business the past ten years, today's tokenizers ‚Äì for example, ingestion companies like Reducto, Heron Data, or Pulse ‚Äì have the potential to become powerful data skeleton keys and eliminate millions of hours of tedious work.

It is becoming nearly impossible to know what is human or real, amplifying existing trust issues across digital networks. Is a payment request legitimate? Is that an email from a colleague or an AI phishing attempt? Is that post from a real public figure or a deepfake? Was that memecoin truly launched by the President? Making sense of this brave new world will require a lot more identity tokens ‚Äì and far better systems to issue, verify, and process them. We wrote a letter to you about this last year; each day since, the problem (and opportunity) keeps growing.

First, we need to supercharge how we identify and verify humans. We expect TITV ‚Äì Total Identity Token Volume (a term we just made up and reserve the right to never use again) ‚Äì to compound rapidly as identity tokens become embedded in more digital workflows. Today, most companies verify users at sign-up, but few continuously authenticate to ensure that future actions come from the same person. As distinguishing good and bad actors or bots grows harder, we expect digital inboxes (e.g. email, SMS, social, wallets, work tools) to move toward requiring cryptographically verifiable signatures for user actions. This may sound extreme but has long been standard where fraud is common or costly, like signing credit card receipts or using 3D Secure for payments.

But simply distinguishing between humans and malicious machines is not enough. The more profound change we need are identity token standards for agents ‚Äì not just to prevent fraud, but to enable trust and collaboration in a world increasingly filled with non-human actors. Of course, machine identity is not new; companies like Microsoft, CyberArk, and SailPoint have worked on it for decades. But basic identifiers like IP addresses and API keys no longer convey what we need to understand about machines' trustworthiness or purpose. For instance, many apps today reject anything that looks like a bot (putting us at the mercy of increasingly baroque CAPTCHAs), when we really need authentication tools that can recognize both good and bad agents everywhere they go.

With early browser agents, we're already seeing the challenge of credentialing agents for basic tasks like ordering lunch or arranging rides. Imagine a more complex use case, like an agent designed to help manage a chronic medical condition by overseeing health metrics, appointments, prescriptions, I/FSA accounts, and more. How do you authorize agents to access medical records? How should providers understand what agents can see or do? How should agents verify provider messages? The identity vectors are complex; for example, you likely want an agent to refill prescriptions, you might want it to consent to new prescriptions ‚Äì but you likely don't want it to unilaterally change your address. To trust agents at a level that makes them most valuable (as much or more than we trust humans), we will need rich, cryptographically attested credentials and human roots of trust.

## Agents may, like people, thrive most when they have resources and freedom

In 1993, Dee Hock, the founder of Visa, described the organization as a ‚Äúchaordic‚Äù system ‚Äì part chaos, part order. It reflected Hock's view that the most resilient and scalable systems (in fields as wide-ranging as business, biology, and technology) are adaptive, distributed, and self-governing. The concept has always been part of the Ribbit lexicon, in part because of how powerful it proved to be. By aligning incentives across a far-flung network, Hock and his team turned the individual actions of stakeholders around the world into a global payment system that handles $16T in annual volume.

There are, of course, lots of other examples of the power of open systems. Some of the world's most prevalent digital infrastructure ‚Äì from Git to PostgreSQL to TensorFlow ‚Äì evolved through permissionless collaboration. Open-source software has become the backbone of cloud computing, machine learning, and the internet itself; Linux, which Steve Ballmer once called a "cancer", now runs 95% of all cloud infrastructure. This letter has mainly focused on centralized efforts to build token factories and agents. Instead of controlling systems, what might be the upside of empowering agents with greater degrees of freedom? ## The era of tokens will spawn surprising new markets, including around attention and truth

Charlie Munger passed away almost exactly a year after ChatGPT was born, so we'll never know what one of the great business minds of the last century might have thought of the Token Revolution. Fortunately, from *Poor Charlie's Almanack* to posthumous Charlie GPTs (like [this one](https://chat.openai.com/g/g-411a7f05-aa72-4753-9ac3-0b0439e7102e-charlie-munger)), we can still borrow a few of his famous mental models. One of our favorites at Ribbit has always been ‚Äúinvert, always invert,‚Äù which Munger adopted from mathematician Carl Gustav Jacobi. So, what counterintuitive question might Munger ask today? We suspect he might sit back (a book or three in hand), listen to all this unbridled enthusiasm, and ask: if AI is going to usher in all this abundance, what will turn out to be scarce?

We can see the utility of this prompt looking backwards. Want to create value in an era of mass production, globalization, and falling prices? One path is to be the fastest runner on an accelerating treadmill; Jeff Bezos has held a masterclass in how to give customers cheaper, better and faster. But another way is to see that in a world flooded with goods, value will accrue to products crafted with obsessive precision and priced accordingly ‚Äì voila, Bernard Arnault is not far behind Bezos in net worth. New waves of abundance open paths to value through scarcity.

AI will make all kinds of physical resources scarce, like energy (a possible subject for the next Ribbit essay!). But channeling Munger and inversion, two less obvious things stand out. The first ‚Äì as you may have noticed in the 10 times you've checked Twitter since starting this letter (each time finding a dozen new threads of LLM-optimized click bait) ‚Äì is attention. When human inspiration is no longer required to generate engaging content, the people, brands, and narratives that can sustain our attention will only grow more valuable. The second, related to the first, is truth. Digitization has broken down information barriers but also unleashed a flood of noise and misinformation. Today, nearly half the people around the world report low to no trust in news media. We can all feel the growing gap in our ability to rely on what we read and hear.

Markets to fill these voids may be the most unheralded opportunities of the next decade. Of course, we already have market-based solutions for both attention (e.g. digital ad markets like Meta or TikTok) and truth (e.g. newspapers pledging objectivity). But these solutions are limited by proprietary use cases and incentives, not to mention flagging trust; they have shown us how you can price a split second of human attention but also how opaque systems are gamed by participants and platforms. Most importantly, these markets are not designed for a world of agents. As the noise grows deafening ‚Äì with agents streaming content 24/7 to hyper-targeted niches ‚Äì we, too, will need agents to help us discern truth and allocate our attention. To do their job, those agents will in turn need information sources that are real-time and trustworthy.

## To Sum It All Up

You have heard us say that we are witnessing profound shifts in knowledge, power, and money, and the relationship among the three. When the printing press, steam engines, and fiat banking collided in the 17th and 18th centuries, the Industrial Revolution changed global power structures and reshaped how people lived; the factories of that era made the physical materials and goods that built the modern world. Today, we see a parallel convergence, with increasingly powerful AI, an explosion in novel energy technologies, and new global systems of money; the factories of this era will create the building blocks of intelligence and once again reshape how we live. To us, this new era is the Token Revolution.

Which leaves us asking: what kind of revolution will it be? Who will win and who will lose? Ribbit exists to back rebels fighting the Status Quo ‚Äì the institutions and industries that exploit customers and impede the flow of innovation to perpetuate their own interests. We believe when you unleash that flow - of electrons, broadband, bits, funds ‚Äì you maximize human potential and make life better.

When we started Ribbit, we knew that no industry was guiltier of taking advantage of customers than financial services. In striving to change money, you begin to see it as the keystone of the Status Quo ‚Äì a literal ledger of who owns and owes what, often accessible only to the privileged few. Finance has long been malleable in theory (especially in the information age ‚Äì it's all 1s and 0s!), but in practice remained brittle, closed, and highly resistant to change. Then rebel founders made a huge dent, bringing digital money to billions of people; now they are working on delivering the tools and advice for people to harness that money. The impact can be profound, since money lies at the heart of everything we do: how we finance our dreams, save for our families, and invest towards our goals. In a digital world, it is becoming a substrate embedded in every product and industry.

But finance is just the tip of the spear ‚Äì in the years ahead, rebels will challenge incumbents in energy, telecom, healthcare, logistics, and every other industry that loses sight of the customer. Tokens will be their weapons of choice. The Status Quo relies on scale, lock-in effects, regulatory capture, and information asymmetry to prevent change, but agents are a powerful antidote. The spread of mobile computing has given billions of people the ability to send and receive tokens and access agents ‚Äì superpowers that will soon turn every interaction and transaction into a fair fight. Small teams of innovators will bring value to billions of customers, and those customers will have tools to find them.

We have made a lot of progress since the start of Ribbit, but there is much more to be done. The weapons for the next stage of the battle are here; they can be wielded for good by rebels fighting for people, or by the Status Quo to continue its campaign of control by exclusion, obfuscation, and regulation. The Status Quo often brandishes human expertise, manipulates financial systems, and husbands our data to perpetuate its agenda. But the Token Revolution has the potential to democratize know-how (expert tokens!), allow us greater control over financial instruments (asset tokens!), and give us the ability to make our data work for us (identity tokens!).
