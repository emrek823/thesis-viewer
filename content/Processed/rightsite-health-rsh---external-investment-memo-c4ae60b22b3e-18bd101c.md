**Executive Summary**

RSH contracts directly with payers as an in-network credentialed provider, builds relationships with local EMS agencies, and provides EMS professionals with on-demand telehealth access to ED physicians and non-clinical, empathetically-trained care navigators. The RSH Connect Platform reduces avoidable ED visits by providing patients with in-network, non-ED sites of care and 24-hour follow-up care.

ED visits and costs have been rising over the last decade - overwhelming EMS agencies, leading to overcrowding in hospitals, driving unnecessary costs for payers, and putting undue burden on patients with OOP costs. Recent trends, however, are bringing all stakeholders to a breaking point. With 74M avoidable ED visits in 2022 and average savings of ~$2,000, we estimate a ~$150B market

**Market Overview**

**ED Costs Increasing but ~50% of EMS Transports Avoidable:** The number of ED visits across the country has increased dramatically over the last decade - up 18% between 2009 (127.3M) and 2019 (150.7M). While there was a 45% decline in overall ED volume during COVID, volumes today are only down 4% compared to 2019. We estimate there will be ~145M total ED visits in the US in 2022. During this same time period, the total spending on ED visits across the country has increased 51% per capita. The average cost for an ED visit can range from ~$500-$600 to $3,400 (RSH ClosedLoop.ai Analysis based on 2019 NYC Medicare data), depending on the geography, payer, and patient characteristics. We will use $2,400 as a baseline for the cost of an avoidable ED admission, and discuss different assumptions in the "Business Model Breakdown" section.

The 16M avoidable ED visits that arrive by ambulance are RSH's initial target with their inbound service, but RSH can expand over time to address all avoidable ED visits.

**EMS Overview:** In 2020, there were 42.5M EMS responses from 911 calls and 30.9M EMS transports (e.g., ~27% of calls don't result in a transport). There are roughly 20K EMS agencies across the country and ~1M licensed EMS professionals (emergency medical responder, emergency medical technician, paramedic, etc). For 911 response services, agencies utilize two types of ambulance unit crews: Advanced Life Support (ALS) units and Basic Life Support (BLS) units. ALS units, which are staffed by two paramedics or one paramedic and a lower level EMT, are equipped with high-acuity life support equipment such as cardiac monitors, defibrillators and oxygen delivery systems, and carry pharmaceutical and medical supplies. BLS units are generally staffed by two lower-level EMTs and are outfitted with medical supplies and equipment necessary to administer first aid and basic medical treatment. Across the country, 30% of EMS calls were serviced by fire departments, 25% were serviced by private companies, 12% were serviced by hospital-owned services, and the other 33% were serviced by community non-profits or governmental, non-fire agencies that only offer EMS.

**EMS Overview:** In 2020, there were 42.5M EMS responses from 911 calls and 30.9M EMS transports (e.g., ~27% of calls don't result in a transport). There are roughly 20K EMS agencies across the country and ~1M licensed EMS professionals (emergency medical responder, emergency medical technician, paramedic, etc). For 911 response services, agencies utilize two types of ambulance unit crews: Advanced Life Support (ALS) units and Basic Life Support (BLS) units. ALS units, which are staffed by two paramedics or one paramedic and a lower level EMT, are equipped with high-acuity life support equipment such as cardiac monitors, defibrillators and oxygen delivery systems, and carry pharmaceutical and medical supplies. BLS units are generally staffed by two lower-level EMTs and are outfitted with medical supplies and equipment necessary to administer first aid and basic medical treatment. Across the country, 30% of EMS calls were serviced by fire departments, 25% were serviced by private companies, 12% were serviced by hospital-owned services, and the other 33% were serviced by community non-profits or governmental, non-fire agencies that only offer EMS.

**Clinical / Product**

**CMS' ET3 Model:** Emergency Triage, Treat, and Transport (ET3) is a voluntary, five-year payment model, launched by CMS (first performance period began Jan 1st, 2021), that provides greater flexibility to ambulance care teams to address non-emergency health care needs of Medicare FFS beneficiaries following a 911 call. Participants in the ET3 Model will receive payment for ambulance services for transport to alternative destinations at the appropriate emergency Basic Life Support (BLS-E) or emergency Advance Life Support, Level 1 (ALS1-E) rate. ET3 attempted to address the financial incentive for EMS but failed to address practical issues of setting up the navigator service for SDoH issues, contracting with payers, and identifying and coordinating with alternate site locations. RSH directly addresses these shortcomings with its approach.

**RSH Inbound Model:** RSH is looking to scale an ET3-like payment model around the country for both government and commercial payers. RSH's model has 5 main components:

*   **911 initiated service:** Patient calls 911 dispatch and fire/ambulance arrives at home.
*   **Telehealth initiation:** Medic determines non-emergency under normal clinical protocol. There is no requirement for medics to check insurance or eligibility for a patient. For example, in Maryland, EMT's already assign clinical priority 1 through 4 for patients based on strict protocols. They would initiate a RSH

**Payer Contracting Strategy:** In the early days, regional rollouts are more likely due to the local nature of the EMS providers in each county, the limited number of active plans, and RSH's contracting strategy of being a local in-network credentialed provider for speed to contract with payers. During initial conversations with payers, RSH learned that payers value clean attribution in a defined episode, the fact that no additional work is required from the payer to achieve savings, and that RSH is guaranteeing the plan won't lose money.

*   **Clean attribution in a defined episode:** In some population health models looking to reduce unnecessary costs, attribution can be difficult, especially when treating patients with chronic disease. If a payer reduces total cost of care for a CHF patient over a year, for example, it may be difficult to directly attribute the savings to nurse case manager that engaged the patient after discharge, the PCP that checked their blood pressure during follow-up, or telehealth and connected weight scale for in-home care. RSH owns the entire well-defined episode from 911 dispatch to final destination during a matter of hours, not weeks or months. This clean attribution is a rarity in many VBC models.
*   **No additional work:** EMS agencies are typically out-of-network and not a group that payers are used to engaging and contracting with. RSH provides payers with a high-quality network of EMS providers and doesn't ask for any additional services from the payer to identify, stratify, or engage patients.
*   **Guarantee:** The gainshare is never paid by the plan unless the RSH diversion is measurable, which is straightforward given clean and fast attribution mentioned above. With its gainshare model, RSH can guarantee savings for a payer with little economic risk to RSH.

RSH is employing what the company describes as a "wholesale" and "retail" strategy for engaging payers.

*   **Retail:** Go to plans in a region where they've engaged EMS agencies and go to payers in the area 1x1, starting with payers with the highest market share. As a local in-network credentialed provider, RSH has the opportunity to bill
