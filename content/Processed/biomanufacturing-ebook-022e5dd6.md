### Quality
Contamination control is the paramount concern in the aseptic filling process, with guidelines put forth by the EMA, FDA, and other organizations requiring all injectable medicines to be sterile. However, throughout the complex fill process, there are significant contamination risks from multiple sources, including human operators, materials, equipment, and the environment.

Recent updates to the quality document known as Annex 1 include much-needed updates to standards in sterile product manufacturing and development. The updates require a multidisciplinary approach to microbial control, which needs to be implemented across all areas of production. The only way to assure quality is by using a well-designed, validated, and controlled manufacturing process.

Over the years, the realm of aseptic filling has made significant advances, including the introduction of restricted access barrier systems (RABS) and gloved isolators. The human element in the filling process has been the biggest risk in the process by introducing opportunities for contamination and inconsistency. The use of robotics eliminates the need for operator interventions, which in other systems could mean the use of gloves that limit dexterity. Robotics also improve the reproducibility of results by increasing filling accuracy and consistency, enabling high product quality and yield.

### THE RESILIENCE STAGE-GATE APPROACH
A technique used by project managers primarily in product development, the stage-gate approach was designed for process improvement.³ Using this approach, a large project is divided into stages. Gates between each stage serve as decision points. At each stage gate, decision makers come together to assess the previous stage. If all objectives from the previous stage have been met, the project moves to the next stage.

## Revised Annex 1 and the Focus on Microbial Control Strategies

### Revised Annex 1 and the Focus on Microbial Control Strategies

The updates to EU Annex 1 added a great deal of emphasis on microbial control. By performing gap assessments and developing thorough, site-specific contamination control strategies, companies can maintain compliance and help ensure the manufacture of sterile products.

### INTRODUCTION
In sterile product manufacturing, current good manufacturing practices (cGMPs) are crucial for basic safety, and compliance is a business imperative. Adhering to cGMPs can help companies maintain customer trust and avoid financial loss. Fortunately, there are published, accessible documents that can help guide companies in maintaining an ethical environment and safe-for-use products.

One notable GMP guideline, Annex 1, is a European Commission regulatory document focusing on sterile product manufacturing. In 2023, EU Annex 1 updates were implemented, causing the guideline's length to more than double. The last version was from 2009 and went through a significant revision process, where a first draft was published for public comment in December 2017. This initial draft showed a change in regulatory thinking, including a stronger emphasis on Quality Risk Management (QRM) principles, contamination control, and the use of modern technologies. Thousands of comments were sent to the European Medicines Agency (EMA), and another draft was made available on February 18, 2020, this time to a targeted audience for final commenting. The final and current version became effective on August 25, 2022.

### Revised Annex 1 and the Focus on Microbial Control Strategies

### OVERVIEW OF THE ANNEX 1 REVISION
These updates cover many overlooked issues in sterile product manufacturing and development, but some topics are highlighted more than others. While it is imperative for companies to tailor their technology, equipment, and protocols to best adhere to cGMPs, the Annex 1 revision also calls for a heavier focus on preventing microbial contamination.

Sterile product manufacturing necessitates a multidisciplinary approach to microbial control, which should be implemented across all production areas. QRM principles and contamination control strategies (CCS) are two major factors that deter microbial contamination. These concepts are best put into practice by understanding the importance of using appropriate technologies, such as isolators, restricted access barrier systems (RABS), and robotic systems, to reduce the risk of product contamination.

Annex 1 requires companies to consider their own contamination controls and risks to understand where their microbial risks exist in processes and products. This helps companies mitigate those risks appropriately to ensure that the best quality products are manufactured and delivered to patients. To achieve this goal, companies should curate a customized approach to microbial control for each site, as risk areas may vary throughout the company. The Annex 1 revision not only pushes for reduced product contamination but should subsequently reduce the number of drug shortages related to loss of sterility assurance (SA).

### GAP ASSESSMENTS
#### Planning Gap Assessments
Organizations have been performing gap assessments of the requirements of the revised Annex 1 to their current practices to develop a remediation plan to mitigate the gaps and ensure compliance. A comprehensive analysis of how this Annex 1 revision compares to the previous version can help organizations—especially multi-site CDMOs—understand what areas require attention and, ultimately, formulate an approach.

At Resilience, the Global Sterility Assurance team took the reins in terms of conducting practical gap assessments by providing each site with a gap assessment template. The Global SA team first assessed any gaps in global standards and then worked with each site to ensure proper interpretation of the intent of each regulation item in Annex 1. Some gaps that will impact all sites at a multi-site company, such as the use of cleanroom socks or banning the use of personal cell phones in cleanrooms, can be mitigated with aligned approaches across all sites. While gaps and at-risk processes vary between these sites, employing the same template across the organization encourages staff to make the most thorough adjustments to meet standards.

### Revised Annex 1 and the Focus on Microbial Control Strategies

#### Communication With Leadership
While the Annex 1 revision's stricter approach to microbial control leads to safer sterile product manufacturing, it can require an investment in capital equipment to comply with the requirements. Communication with leadership is a critical factor in ensuring management awareness of gaps and appropriate resources are allocated to mitigate these gaps. In some companies, aging equipment—such as open filling lines or open RABS that require manual cleaning—will need to be updated to newer technology to meet the requirements of Annex 1, which will require significant capital investment. Complete, company-wide transparency is imperative in linking the results of an initial gap assessment to the creation, establishment, and maintenance of a CCS.

### DEVELOPMENT OF A CONTAMINATION CONTROL STRATEGY
#### Not One-Size-Fits-All
As a result of the Annex 1 revision, all companies making sterile products must create a CCS. CCS is defined in Annex 1 as a planned set of controls for microorganisms, endotoxin/pyrogen, and particles derived from current product and process understanding that assures process performance and product quality. The controls can include parameters and attributes related to active substances, excipient and drug product materials and components, facility and equipment operating conditions, in-process controls, finished product specifications, and the associated methods and frequency of monitoring and control.

The CCS will help companies develop, follow, and maintain acceptable microbial controls to ensure the manufacture of high-quality, sterile products. By carefully reviewing the results of each site's gap assessment, proper strategies can be developed considering processes and products that are most at risk.

### Revised Annex 1 and the Focus on Microbial Control Strategies

#### Consistent Updates
The CCS should consider all aspects of contamination control and requires ongoing and periodic review. While not all the Annex 1 updates are new to the industry, some requirements, such as wearing dedicated cleanroom socks versus outdoor socks in Grades A, B, and C areas, may require some time to implement as companies decide the best way to address the contamination risk. Some companies are risk-justifying not implementing cleanroom socks due to the higher microbial risk of having employees barefoot in changing rooms. Other companies are implementing cleanroom socks and need to determine if they will use reusable laundered socks or one-time-use socks.

This raises the question: Do the contamination-control benefits of single-use socks justify the toll they take on sustainability? Again, this is where companies should adhere to a case-by-case basis and risk assessment. Companies should document their decisions and justifications but ultimately prioritize what is safest in the context of their manufacturing process. A CCS is meant to be maintained as a living document that is frequently updated, meaning that companies should employ good judgment while also understanding that the guidelines may later be revised as new data shapes GMPs.

#### Ensuring Annex 1 Compliance in a Contamination Control Strategy
Resilience ensures Annex 1 compliance by continuously tracking specific products and processes while referencing the most up-to-date versions of all design documents, qualifications, and guidelines to navigate a CCS properly.

Some CDMOs may choose one general facility CCS that applies to all clients, which will be focused on general facility design and controls, and then create additional client- or product-specific CCS documents specific to each client's products and processes.

CDMOs often work around many moving parts; Resilience, for example, has sites in six states in the United States and one in Canada, as well as many different client products and processes.² As stated earlier, to streamline the development of a control strategy, CDMOs should understand that a CCS is not a one-size-fits-all plan. Microbial assessment tools like Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points (HACCP) can be invaluable in designing a fitted control plan and following continuous adherence to procedures that protect each site's most at-risk products or systems.

Additionally, alternative methods that optimize contamination detection, such as modern microbial methods and biofluorescent particle monitoring equipment, can be helpful and are encouraged in Annex 1. Equipment used for the manufacturing and testing of products will protect these products from potential extraneous sources of endotoxin/pyrogen, particulate, and microbial contamination.
